#One-line definition

Our answer to the SHAD 2026 Design–Entrepreneurship brief: harvest the piezoelectric crystal out of discarded disposable lighters, turn it into a contact microphone, and build a distributed sound-awareness system that tells deaf and hard-of-hearing people what their home is doing.

#0 — The strategic frame (read before the rest)

The brief asks: how might we reimagine the life cycle of an everyday good?

Our everyday good is the disposable lighter, not the sound device. The device is what the lighter becomes.

This distinction decides whether we're on-brief. Framed as "assistive technology for deaf people", this answers a different question than the one asked. Framed as "the disposable lighter is a linear-life-cycle object that ends as hazardous waste, and here's the loop that closes it" — the assistive device becomes the payoff that proves the loop is worth closing.

Lead with the lighter. Land on the device. (my read — but I'd defend it hard.)

#1 — The technical answer

"How are we going to capture the sound in the microphone to be able to tell AI which sound is which, and how is it transmitting it?"

#1.1 What a piezoelectric element is

Inside a lighter's igniter is a small cylinder of PZT — lead zirconate titanate, a piezoelectric ceramic. Piezoelectricity means deform the crystal, get a voltage; apply a voltage, get a deformation. The two directions are the same effect running forwards and backwards.

In the lighter, a spring-loaded hammer strikes the PZT hard enough to generate several kilovolts, which arcs across the spark gap and lights the butane [verify].

We use the same direction, far more gently. Sound is a pressure wave. Pressure deforms the crystal. The crystal emits a voltage that traces the wave. That is a microphone.

Two properties matter enormously and both come free:

  1. It is self-generating. A piezo needs no power at all to sit there converting vibration to voltage. Compare a MEMS microphone, which needs a constant bias current just to exist. This single fact is what makes our battery life possible — see §1.6.
  2. It is mechanically robust and costs us nothing, because we're pulling it out of the trash.

#1.2 The catch — piezo is a contact mic, and that's our advantage

Air is very bad at pushing on a stiff ceramic cylinder. Solids are very good at it.

So a lighter piezo is a contact microphone: excellent at vibration conducted through a solid, poor at sound travelling through air.

This kills the naive product — one box on a shelf listening to the room — and replaces it with a better one:

The two hardest sounds for this approach are baby crying and dog barking. Both are airborne. It is the first limitation a technical reader will find, so we name it ourselves. Two honest responses:

  • (a) Scope down and re-aim. Ship contact events only, and replace dog barking with smoke and CO alarm detection. A deaf person sleeping through a smoke alarm is a genuine, documented life-safety gap [verify] and a far stronger problem than a barking dog.
  • (b) Hybrid. Bond the piezo to a thin diaphragm (a tin lid, a stretched membrane) so airborne pressure is mechanically collected and coupled into the ceramic — this is how DIY contact mics are pressed into service as air mics. It works, badly. Or accept a ~$1 [est] MEMS mic on the two airborne SKUs and keep piezo everywhere else.

Recommendation: (a) now, (b) as roadmap. A narrow product that never lies to the user beats a broad one that cries wolf. (my read)

#1.3 The analogue front end, stage by stage

[piezo] → [protection] → [buffer/preamp] → [anti-alias filter] → [ADC] → [MCU]

① Protection. A hard knock can drive the piezo to tens of volts — that's the whole point of the component in its original job. A microcontroller input dies above roughly 3.6 V. So: a series resistor (~1 kΩ) and two clamping diodes, one to the 3.3 V rail and one to ground, which shunt anything outside the safe range. Cheap, small, non-negotiable. (This is the same protective instinct as the flyback/clamp reasoning on mosfet-tether-trigger-circuit.)

② Buffer / preamp. A piezo has enormous output impedance — megohms — and can supply almost no current. Wire it directly to a microcontroller ADC, whose input impedance is in the kilohms, and the ADC loads the source: most of the signal disappears and the low frequencies vanish first. So the first active stage must present a very high input impedance:

  • a JFET source-follower (2N5457) or a rail-to-rail CMOS op-amp (MCP6002),
  • with a bias resistor around 10 MΩ to set the DC operating point without loading the signal,
  • gain of roughly 20–100× [est], tuned per unit because reclaimed elements vary (see §4).

Also: the piezo signal swings both positive and negative around zero, but an ADC only reads 0 V to 3.3 V. So we bias the whole signal up to mid-rail (1.65 V) and treat that as the new zero. Negative excursions become "below 1.65 V."

③ Anti-alias filter. A low-pass filter set just under half the sampling rate. This is the Nyquist limit: a sampler at rate fs can only faithfully represent frequencies below fs/2. Anything above that doesn't get ignored — it folds back down into the audible band as a phantom tone at the wrong frequency. Skip the filter and the classifier will confidently identify sounds that were never in the room.

Sampling at 16 kHz → filter above ~7 kHz.

④ ADC and MCU. Sample at 16 kHz, 12–16 bit. That faithfully captures everything up to 8 kHz, which covers doorbells, knocks, alarms, and the fundamental plus lower harmonics of a baby's cry.

Chip: ESP32-C3 — built-in ADC, built-in Bluetooth LE and WiFi, roughly $2.50 [est], and critically it is sold as a pre-certified radio module, which lets us inherit its regulatory certification instead of paying for our own. That last point is a finance decision disguised as an engineering one — see §3.

#1.4 How the AI tells one sound from another

This is the part that usually gets hand-waved. Here is what actually happens.

We never hand the AI a raw waveform. A 16 kHz stream is 16,000 numbers per second with no structure a classifier can grab onto. Instead we transform it into something with visible shape:

Step 1 — Frame it. Cut the stream into short, overlapping windows: 25 ms long, stepping 10 ms. Sound changes slowly enough that within 25 ms it's effectively frozen; the overlap means we never miss an event that straddles a boundary.

Step 2 — FFT each window. A Fast Fourier Transform converts a window from amplitude over time into energy at each frequency. We stop asking "what was the pressure at each instant" and start asking "how much 400 Hz was there, how much 3 kHz."

Step 3 — Collapse onto the mel scale. Human hearing resolves low frequencies finely and high frequencies coarsely — the difference between 200 and 300 Hz is obvious, between 8,000 and 8,100 Hz is not. The mel scale mimics that curve, collapsing ~256 raw frequency bins into ~40 perceptual bands. This discards detail we don't need and shrinks the model by an order of magnitude.

Step 4 — Stack the windows into a picture. Time on the x-axis, mel band on the y-axis, energy as brightness. This is a mel spectrogram, and it is literally an image.

And this is the whole trick: each sound class has a visually distinctive picture.

  • A doorbell — two or three clean horizontal bars (near-pure tones) with a slow exponential decay.
  • A knock — sharp vertical stripes: broadband energy for a few milliseconds, near-silence between, repeating at roughly 3–5 Hz [est]. Nothing else in a house looks like that.
  • A smoke alarm — a hard narrow band around 3.2 kHz [verify] pulsing in a fixed three-beat pattern (the T-3 temporal pattern) [verify].
  • A baby cry — a wavering fundamental around 300–600 Hz [verify] with a stack of harmonics above it, rising and falling over seconds.

Step 5 — Classify the picture. A small convolutional neural network — the same architecture family used for image recognition — reads the spectrogram and outputs one probability per class. Quantised to 8-bit integers it fits in a few hundred kilobytes and runs on the ESP32 in tens of milliseconds [est].

Tooling: TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers, or Edge Impulse if we want a GUI and only have eight days.

Step 6 — Debounce, hard. Never alert on a single frame. Require the model to exceed ~0.85 confidence across 3 consecutive windows before firing, then suppress repeat alerts for ~10 seconds.

False positives are the single biggest reason assistive alerting devices end up in a drawer [verify — but I'd bet on it]. Being half a second slow is invisible. Being wrong destroys trust permanently.

Training data — the honest gap. ESC-50 and Google's AudioSet are free labelled environmental-sound datasets [verify]. But they were recorded with air microphones, and our contact-mic recordings will look measurably different — different frequency emphasis, different noise floor, no room reverb. So a pretrained model won't transfer cleanly. We'd need to record our own dataset: roughly 100–500 examples per class [est], plus augmentation.

That's a real week of work we do not have yet. We state it plainly rather than implying the model already exists.

#1.5 How it transmits — the decision that defines the product

Two architectures. Choosing correctly is most of our differentiation.

#Option A — stream the audio, classify elsewhere ❌

The node continuously transmits raw audio; the phone or a cloud server recognises it.

  • 16 kHz × 16-bit = 256 kbit/s, continuously. Battery life in hours, not months.
  • Requires WiFi. Dies in a power cut, dies on a flaky router.
  • And the real disqualifier: it is a live, always-open microphone in a deaf person's home, streaming to a server they cannot audit. Sold to a user who by definition cannot hear the device operating, on grounds of safety. That is an unacceptable trade, so we rule it out rather than waiting for someone else to raise it.

#Option B — classify on the node, transmit only the answer ✅

The node does everything itself and emits a tiny message:

{ "node": "front-door", "event": "knock", "conf": 0.94, "t": 1753280000 }

Roughly 60 bytes [est]. Over Bluetooth LE that's a few milliseconds of radio time.

Path: node → BLE GATT notification → phone app (background, no screen needed) → phone relays to the paired smartwatch → haptic buzz + text on the wrist.

The watch is the correct endpoint. It's on the body, it vibrates, and for a deaf user a wrist tap is the notification channel that actually works — a phone face-down on a table is useless. Assign a distinct vibration pattern per event class and users learn to identify the sound by feel, without looking at anything.

Range caveat: BLE is roughly 10 m indoors through walls [verify]. For a whole house either the phone is the hub and must be nearby, or one mains-powered node acts as a WiFi bridge and the battery nodes mesh to it over BLE. The bridge is the better product and deserves its own SKU. [est]

#1.6 Power budget — why this is even possible

The obvious objection: continuously sampling and running a neural net means the MCU never sleeps, drawing ~20–25 mA [est], so a 1000 mAh battery lasts about two days. That would be fatal.

The fix is a two-stage wake, and the piezo makes it free.

Because a piezo is self-generating (§1.1), it produces a voltage with no power supplied to it. So we let an ultra-low-power analogue comparator — a few microamps — watch the piezo output against a threshold, while the MCU sits in deep sleep. Sound arrives → comparator trips → MCU wakes in ~10 ms → captures ~1 s → classifies → transmits → returns to sleep.

StateCurrentDuration
Deep sleep + comparator~10 µA [est]~always
Wake, capture, infer, transmit~25 mA [est]~1.5 s per event

At 50 events/day [est]:

  • active: 50 × 1.5 s × 25 mA ≈ 0.52 mAh/day
  • sleeping: 10 µA × 24 h = 0.24 mAh/day
  • total ≈ 0.76 mAh/day → a 1000 mAh cell lasts well over a year, at which point self-discharge, not our circuit, is the limit.
Watch out

Honest caveat: a CR2032 coin cell holds ~220 mAh but cannot deliver 25 mA pulses without its voltage collapsing [verify]. So either a small LiPo, or a coin cell with a supercapacitor to buffer the transmit bursts. This is a real design constraint, not a detail — get it wrong and the demo browns out on stage.

Trade-off to state plainly: the comparator wake loses the first ~10 ms of every sound. For a knock (repeated impulses) or an alarm (repeated beeps) that's irrelevant — the trigger is the onset and the following pattern is what gets classified. For a single isolated transient it would matter. (my read)

#2 — Stakeholders and target audience

#2.1 Primary users are not one audience

Roughly 3.2 million Canadians have some degree of hearing loss [verify — Statistics Canada; check both the figure and the definition of "hearing loss" it uses before relying on it, because the number swings enormously depending on threshold].

That population contains at least four groups who want different things:

① Culturally Deaf — ASL/LSQ users, "Deaf" with a capital D. Deafness is an identity and a linguistic community, not a medical deficit. They do not want to be fixed and will reject a product that implies they should be. They do want access to information they're currently locked out of. Frame this as information access, never as correction or cure.

② Late-deafened adults — lost hearing after acquiring spoken language. Often the most frustrated segment: they know precisely what they're missing, and existing products feel patronising.

③ Seniors with age-related loss — the largest group by headcount, the least likely to adopt unfamiliar technology, and the most likely to have the product bought for them by an adult child. Design for the buyer and the user simultaneously — they're different people with different criteria.

④ Hard-of-hearing people who remove hearing aids at night — badly underserved, and strategically the most interesting. They are functionally deaf for eight hours a day, in exactly the window when a smoke alarm matters most.

Also name explicitly: deaf parents of hearing infants. That's the baby-cry case, and it's the most immediately legible scenario in the whole case.

#2.2 The thing most student teams get wrong

The Deaf community has a long history of hearing people building things for them without asking. Our language will be read closely and quickly.

  • Say "deaf and hard of hearing." Capital-D Deaf for cultural identity.
  • Never "hearing impaired," "suffers from," "afflicted with."
  • Never frame the product as overcoming, triumphing, or restoring.
  • Co-design. Getting deaf people to review the concept before it is built is the highest-value validation available, and it is the step projects like this most often skip.

#3.3 Wholesale and channel pricing

The rule: in consumer hardware you do not have a price, you have a price ladder. Everyone between us and the user takes a cut, and MSRP is only what the last person in the chain charges.

The industry convention is keystone — a retailer buys at ~50% of MSRP and sells at 100%. If a distributor sits above the retailer ("two-tier"), they take a further cut, so we sell at ~40% of MSRP. [verify — these are standard consumer-electronics conventions, worth one source]

#The finding that matters

Our MSRP is only 3.55× COGS at launch volume. Consumer hardware generally wants 4–5× to survive a full channel [verify]. That single ratio drives three conclusions:

① Do not offer a two-tier distributor deal in year one. At $14.00 against $9.85 COGS we gross $4.15 — and after the allowances below that's roughly $2 a unit. We would be manufacturing, warranting, and supporting a product for pocket change while carrying all the risk.

② The invoice price is not what we receive. Wholesale deals carry deductions that a first-time founder always forgets:

DeductionTypicalOn a $17.50 invoice
RMA / returns allowance2–3%−$0.53
Co-op marketing allowance2–5%−$0.53
Freight (we usually pay inbound)—−$1.00
Net-net received≈ $15.44

So real keystone margin at launch volume is $5.59, not $7.65 — 36%, not 44%. Always work from net-net; invoice price overstates what the business actually receives.

③ Volume fixes it, and nothing else does. At 10k units COGS falls to $6.50 and keystone margin nearly doubles to $11.00. Our route to a healthy channel runs through volume, not through price increases — and the institutional/landlord channel (§2.3) is what gets us there, because it buys in blocks of 50–200 rather than ones and twos.

#Two more things wholesale will do to us

Payment terms are a working-capital trap. Distributors and retailers pay Net 30–60. We pay our suppliers before we build. Ship 1,000 units on Net 60 and we have spent $9,850 on components and wait two months for $15,450. That gap has to be funded or the company dies while profitable — which is why §3.5 puts non-dilutive grants first.

MAP policy. Set a Minimum Advertised Price so a retailer can't discount to $24.99 and undercut our own site. Without it, our direct channel — the only one paying 72% margin — gets destroyed by our own partners.

#The starter kit is currently mispriced ⚠️

3 nodes + bridge at $99.99 against a kit COGS of ~$40.05 [est] (3 × $9.85 + ~$10.50 for a mains-powered bridge) is only 2.5×. At keystone that's $50.00 − $40.05 = $9.95, a 20% margin. That doesn't survive contact with a channel.

Three ways out — pick one:

  • Raise the kit to $139.99 (3.5×, matches single-node economics), or
  • Cut it to 2 nodes + bridge at $99.99 (~3.3×), or
  • Keep $99.99 as direct-only and never wholesale the bundle — bundles as a DTC-exclusive margin play is a legitimate, common strategy.

Recommendation: 2 nodes + bridge at $99.99. It holds the psychologically important sub-$100 price point, fixes the ratio, and a 2-node starter (door + smoke alarm) is the honest entry configuration anyway. (my read)

#3.4 The costs teams forget

Radio certification (ISED Canada + FCC). Certifying our own radio design: $5,000–15,000 [est]. Using a pre-certified module and inheriting its certification: ≈ $0. This is why §1.3's chip choice is a finance decision. It belongs in the financial analysis rather than the technical one — this is where hardware startups actually die.

Safety certification — the strategic fork. The moment we claim "detects your smoke alarm" as a life-safety function, we are plausibly in UL/ULC territory: tens of thousands of dollars and many months [verify]. Two roads:

  • market it as a convenience notifier, stay outside that regime, ship fast, weaker claim;
  • or invest, certify, and own the strongest claim in the category.

There is no free version. The fork is named here rather than glossed over, because it changes the cost model materially.

Also budget for: liability insurance, electronics/materials compliance, and safe handling of residual butane (§4) — which is the one line item that could actually injure someone.

#3.5 Funding path

Grants before equity:

  1. Non-dilutive first — Mitacs, NSERC, Ontario Centre of Innovation, plus accessibility-specific funds
  2. A funded pilot with a landlord or a community organisation
  3. Seed round against pilot data

This mirrors a trajectory presented to the cohort by a SHAD speaker — student idea → DMZ → city partnership → OVIN funding. That is a documented precedent for this kind of venture.

#4 — Logistics — how the product is made

This section is where we win the brief. Everything else describes a good product. This is the part that answers "reimagine the life cycle of an everyday good."

#4.1 The supply chain is a waste stream

Disposable lighters are a known problem at waste facilities: not recyclable, classified as household hazardous waste, and residual butane makes them a fire and explosion risk in compactors and sorting lines [verify — this needs a real citation; the entire supply-chain argument rests on it, so it is the highest-priority fact to source].

So we do not buy transducers. We remove a fire hazard from municipal waste and get a free sensor out of it.

Partner with municipal HHW depots, which currently pay to dispose of these. That inverts the normal startup problem: our input has negative cost to its current owner.

#4.3 Two things to be honest about

① Reclaimed parts are inconsistent. Every harvested piezo has slightly different sensitivity — different age, different manufacturer, different wear. Sorting into grades handles the spread; it doesn't eliminate it.

Our real answer is in firmware, not in sorting: each node self-calibrates on first boot — records ~10 seconds of ambient, establishes its own noise floor, sets its own gain and comparator threshold. That converts a manufacturing variability problem into a software problem we've already solved.

This is the difference between a concept and a design: the failure mode is anticipated rather than discovered later. (my read)

② PZT contains lead. Lead zirconate titanate is substantially lead oxide by mass [verify]. There is a RoHS exemption for piezoelectric ceramics [verify] — so this is legal, and diverting it from landfill is genuinely better than the status quo.

But we must show we handle it responsibly: no grinding, no dust generation, element sealed inside the enclosure, and recovered again at end of life.

We raise this ourselves rather than leaving it to be discovered. Same logic as owning the airborne-sound weakness in §1.2.

#4.4 Closing the loop twice

  • The lighter body is polypropylene. Regrind it into our enclosure and the same discarded object supplies both the sensor and its housing. That's the loop closing on itself, and it's a strong visual for the deck.
  • Design for disassembly — screws, not glue. If our own product can't be taken apart and reclaimed, we are doing the exact thing the brief criticises. This is also the Right to Repair hook the brief explicitly supplies.
  • Prepaid return mailer at end of life. This is the same take-back mechanic worked out for the earlier Re:Ring concept, carried across.

#4.5 Manufacturing at low volume

Hand assembly through a partner social enterprise or supported-employment workshop. Slower per unit, higher labour cost — and it keeps the whole story coherent: waste diverted locally, labour local, nothing shipped across an ocean to save forty cents. A sustainability claim does not survive a "circular" product being assembled 11,000 km away.

#5 — Marketing

#5.1 Positioning

Avoid "now you can hear your home" — it frames deafness as lack, and segment ① (§2.1) will bounce off it instantly.

Use: "Know what your home is doing." Information, not correction.

And the earned-media line, which is where sustainability meets accessibility:

"We make hearing accessibility out of hazardous waste."

That is a press story a conventional assistive-device company cannot buy, and it's the one sentence that ties the product back to the brief.

#5.2 Channel — do not go cold direct-to-consumer

The Deaf community is tight-knit, institutionally organised, and strongly referral-driven [verify — but consistent with how minority-language communities generally adopt products]. A single endorsement from Canadian Hearing Services will outweigh a large advertising budget.

Sequence:

  1. Community institutions — Canadian Hearing Services, Deaf clubs, ASL organisations
  2. Audiology clinics — reach people at the moment of diagnosis, when they're actively looking
  3. Provincial funding vendor lists (Ontario ADP) — see §2.3
  4. Landlord / property-manager B2B for accessible units
  5. Direct-to-consumer last, powered by the credibility the first four generate

#5.3 Execution rules

  • Every asset captioned. No exceptions.
  • ASL-first video with Deaf presenters — not hearing actors signing. The community can tell immediately, and getting it wrong costs more trust than the video generates.
  • Demo the product silently. If our advertisement requires sound to make sense, we've misunderstood our own customer. This is also a genuinely striking creative constraint.
  • No inspiration porn. No swelling music over someone "overcoming" anything.

#5.4 The pricing message is marketing

"No subscription for safety alerts" is a trust argument, not a pricing footnote. It says: we will not hold your safety hostage to a recurring payment. Lead with it, don't bury it.

#6 — Resource management

The phrase is ambiguous and both readings are askable. Cover both.

#6.1 Material and environmental resources

Do a mass balance per unit: grams of PZT diverted from landfill, grams of virgin plastic avoided, one lighter removed from a waste stream where it is an active fire hazard.

Watch out

Do not claim a carbon win we cannot defend. Collecting, transporting, purging, and disassembling a lighter plausibly consumes more energy than manufacturing a new $0.15 piezo element [est] (my read). If someone else does that arithmetic and we haven't, every other claim loses credibility by association.

Our defensible claims are waste diversion and hazard removal. Both are true, both are strong, neither requires a contested carbon number.

If we do want a real figure rather than a gesture, A SHAD carbon-accounting workshop (Week 3) is the right method source.

#7 — What makes us different

#7.1 Name the competition first

Any honest comparison has to start with them:

CompetitorReality
Apple Sound RecognitionBuilt into every iPhone and Apple Watch. Free. Already detects doorbells, knocking, dog barking, baby crying [verify — check the current class list]
Google Sound NotificationsSame, on Android. Free.
Sonic Alert, Bellman & Symfon, Silent CallDedicated assistive alerting hardware, ~$100–400/device [est]
Ring / NestDoorbell-specific phone alerts

The case below is why we still think there's room. (my read)

#7.2 The five real differentiators

① Contact sensing, not room sensing. ← the load-bearing one

Phone-based recognition listens to a room. It needs the phone present, awake, unmuted, and out of a pocket — and it fundamentally cannot distinguish a doorbell on television from the actual doorbell, because to an air microphone they are the same sound.

Our sensor is bolted to the door. It fires when that specific door is physically struck. A TV cannot trigger it. A conversation cannot trigger it. A neighbour's doorbell cannot trigger it.

Combined with §1.4's point that false positives are the primary abandonment cause, this means we are architecturally more reliable, not incrementally more reliable. That's a difference in kind, and it's the one to lead with.

② Distributed, not centralised. At $35/node you instrument the door and the crib and the smoke alarm and the dryer. Incumbent hardware is priced like medical equipment, so households buy one unit and cover one thing.

③ Nothing leaves the room. On-device inference (§1.5) means no audio is ever transmitted. Our cloud-based competitors are, functionally, an always-on microphone inside a deaf person's home. We are not — and not by policy, which can change, but by architecture. There is no stream to intercept because no stream exists.

④ Built from hazardous waste. No competitor can say their transducer was a fire hazard pulled out of a municipal waste depot. This is the only differentiator that also answers the brief, which is why it leads.

⑤ No subscription on the safety path. (§3.2)

#7.3 Own the weakness before they name it

Airborne sounds — baby crying, dog barking — are our weak case. Contact sensing is a genuine trade-off, not a free lunch.

State it plainly, then answer it: we ship the contact classes where we outperform everything on the market, and the hybrid air-sensing node is a costed roadmap item (§1.2b).

Naming the limitation and showing the plan is the honest position, and the only one that survives contact with a real user.

#Open questions

  1. Can a reclaimed lighter piezo actually resolve a knock into a usable signal? Completely untested. The entire project rests on this single assumption. A piezo, an op-amp, an Arduino and an LED settle it.
  2. What is the real citation for lighters causing waste-facility fires? The whole supply-chain narrative (§4.1) hangs off it, and right now it is [verify].
  3. Are alerting/signalling devices covered by Ontario's Assistive Devices Program? Changes the business model materially if yes.
  4. Convenience notifier or certified life-safety device? (§3.4) Still to decide — it changes the financials, the marketing language, and the legal exposure simultaneously.
  5. Review by deaf and hard-of-hearing users before anything is built. The highest-value validation available.
